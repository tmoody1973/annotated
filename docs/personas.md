# Annotated — user personas (beyond Jason)

Four target users, ranked by how directly Annotated's moat serves them. The moat,
restated: **transcript-anchored audio clipping** (drag across transcript words →
the matching ≤90s audio), **automatic source attribution** (every clip links back),
a **fair-use posture** (visible "file a claim" on every annotation), and a **social
feed** with follow / comment / ▲brilliant–▼BS voting.

The thread running through all four: they care about *what was actually said*, they
want it *sourced*, and they want their *take attached to it permanently*. Annotated
turns a fleeting media moment into a citable, shareable, defensible object.

---

## 1. Maya Okafor — Independent podcaster / audio producer

*The home-turf user. If Annotated nails one persona, it's this one.*

- **Role.** Hosts a twice-weekly culture-and-ideas podcast (~15k downloads/episode),
  produces it solo, and writes a weekly station segment that needs guest soundbites.
- **Goals.** Grow the show by riding the conversation around bigger podcasts; never
  lose a quotable 40 seconds; turn audio into social posts without a video editor.
- **Jobs to be done.**
  - "When I hear a killer moment on another show, clip *exactly* that audio and post
    my reaction, so my audience ties my take to the moment."
  - "When I prep, pull a guest's past soundbites from their other appearances."
- **Pains today.** Clipping podcast audio is miserable — scrub a waveform blind, screen-
  record, no transcript to find the line, Spotify locks the file, and nothing attributes
  the source, so sharing feels like theft. Twitter/X clips are video-only.
- **How she uses Annotated.** Opens an episode page (NPR / Simplecast / Apple), drags
  across the transcript to grab the exact span, records a 15-second voice reaction,
  publishes. The landing page links back to the show. She drops the link in her socials.
- **Killer feature.** Transcript-anchored audio clipping — the one thing she literally
  cannot do anywhere else.
- **Day in the life.** Monday show-prep: pulls three guest soundbites for the station
  segment in ten minutes. Wednesday: hears a hot take on a top-10 podcast, clips it,
  voice-reacts, posts — picks up new followers who found the moment through her.
- **What makes her churn.** Top shows that can't be clipped (Spotify-exclusive, no RSS);
  rough audio quality on the clip; attribution that's weak enough to feel like ripping.

## 2. Daniel Reyes — Accountability journalist / media critic

*The differentiation story: "sourced receipts."*

- **Role.** Reporter at a regional digital outlet who also runs a media-criticism
  newsletter. Covers local politics and the national press.
- **Goals.** Capture what public figures *actually* said (not the paraphrase), publish a
  sourced receipt before the cycle moves, stay legally defensible, build authority.
- **Jobs to be done.**
  - "When an official says something revealing on a podcast, clip the exact words,
    sourced, and publish my annotation as a citable receipt — fast."
  - "Keep a paper trail that points at the primary source, not a screenshot that rots."
- **Pains today.** Hand-transcribing audio; screenshotting tweets that later get deleted;
  no clean way to *cite* a spoken claim; quiet anxiety about the legality of clipping.
- **How he uses Annotated.** Clips the segment, writes a tight factual annotation; the
  source-linked page *is* the receipt. The visible claim button signals good faith. The
  ▲/▼ vote surfaces whether the community reads it as solid or spin.
- **Killer feature.** Source-attributed, transcript-exact clips plus the fair-use claim
  posture — defensibility he can stand behind.
- **Day in the life.** A city councilor's podcast remark contradicts last month's vote.
  Clip + annotate + publish in ten minutes; the newsletter embeds the landing page; other
  reporters cite it.
- **What makes him churn.** Anything that makes attribution look like republishing (legal
  exposure); a feed that devolves into a partisan dunk-tank and taints his credibility.

## 3. Dr. Priya Nair — Researcher / educator

*Turns a media moment into a citable teaching object.*

- **Role.** Communications professor; teaches media studies; publishes peer-reviewed work.
- **Goals.** Precise, citable media excerpts for teaching and research; assignable clips;
  a reusable library of annotated moments.
- **Jobs to be done.**
  - "When a 90-second exchange in an interview nails a concept, clip it with an exact
    timestamp and my explanation, and assign it to the seminar."
  - "Produce citations my peers and students actually trust."
- **Pains today.** YouTube timestamp links are fragile and lose context; there's no
  transcript-anchored way to cite audio; screen-recording violates ToS; no annotation
  layer over the source.
- **How she uses Annotated.** Clips lecture / interview / podcast segments, writes an
  explanatory annotation; the source-linked page becomes a shareable, citable teaching
  object. Students follow her feed and comment.
- **Killer feature.** Transcript-anchored precision + a durable, source-linked citable
  artifact.
- **Day in the life.** Builds next week's listening list as six annotated clips; students
  comment and debate; she reuses the same clips next semester.
- **What makes her churn.** Link rot / clips that aren't durable enough to cite; no way to
  organize them (no folders/library — a real gap, currently a v1 non-goal); weak
  attribution that fails an academic-integrity sniff test.

## 4. Marcus Webb — Investor / analyst (the Jason-adjacent)

*Keeps the pitch one degree from Jason's world.*

- **Role.** Early-stage VC / angel who runs a small syndicate, posts takes on X, and
  listens to 10+ hours of tech podcasts a week.
- **Goals.** Build a *public track record of calls*; clip founder/CEO statements, earnings
  remarks, and podcast claims; signal a thesis; earn deal flow through reputation.
- **Jobs to be done.**
  - "When a founder says something revealing on a podcast, clip it and attach my analysis,
    timestamped, so my track record is public and sourced."
  - "Keep receipts on my predictions — and on theirs."
- **Pains today.** Screenshotting transcripts (no audio); takes vanishing into the X feed
  with no permanent, sourced home; no clean artifact that *is* a track record.
- **How he uses Annotated.** Clips the statement, adds a sharp analytical annotation, gets
  ▲/▼ from peers; his profile becomes his track record. Follows other sharp analysts.
- **Killer feature.** A sourced, timestamped clip as a permanent, votable track-record
  entry — accountability for calls, his and others'.
- **Day in the life.** All-In drops; he clips a Chamath claim, annotates a counter-thesis;
  it circulates through the investor corner of the feed and a founder DMs him.
- **What makes him churn.** Cold-start (no audience yet for his takes); a product that
  reads as a toy rather than a credible track-record tool; paywalled/exclusive shows that
  won't clip.

## 5. Renée Castellano — Audience-development editor (institutional / publisher account)

*Not an individual creator — the human who'd run a newspaper's or magazine's account.
The wedge for "publisher accounts," and the move that turns Annotated's IP risk into a moat.*

- **Role.** Owns distribution, referral traffic, and audience growth for a mid-size metro
  newspaper-and-magazine group. Champions new platforms — but must clear the rights/legal
  desk before adopting anything. (So the "user" is really a buying committee: Renée wants
  reach; legal fears exposure.)
- **What the org wants.** Referral traffic back to articles; reach a younger, audio-native
  audience; turn its journalism into shareable, *attributed* social units; reclaim
  distribution from platforms that strip the byline and bury outbound links; audience data.
- **What the org fears (the gatekeeper).** Losing control of how its work is excerpted;
  **cannibalization** (readers consume the 90s clip instead of clicking through);
  misrepresentation in someone else's commentary; setting a bad legal precedent; brand
  safety — their reporting sitting next to toxic takes.
- **Jobs to be done.**
  - "Get our journalism in front of new audiences as sourced, attributed clips that drive
    clicks *back to us*."
  - "Hold a *verified* presence so our official clips aren't impostors."
  - "Manage — not just litigate — how third parties excerpt our content."
  - "See what's resonating and what it sends back in traffic."
- **Pains today.** Their content is clipped and screenshotted everywhere with no attribution
  and no traffic back; aggregators strip them; social platforms deprioritize outbound links;
  they have no foothold on emerging clip-native platforms.
- **How they'd use a publisher account.** A verified org account; staff publish official
  annotated clips of their own pieces (referral traffic); they *claim and manage* third-party
  clips of their content — the claim button as a managed channel, not a takedown war;
  referral-traffic + engagement analytics; optionally featured/curated clip collections.
- **Killer feature.** The verified publisher account + the attribution/claim system reframed
  as a **managed distribution-and-control channel** — the IP risk converted into a traffic
  engine — backed by referral analytics that prove ROI.
- **Day in the life.** Renée's team clips the 90-second crux of an investigation, annotates
  it, publishes from the verified account; it spikes referral readers. She spots a rival's
  clip of their reporting and, instead of a takedown, posts an official response on it.
- **What makes them never adopt (or churn).** Clips that cannibalize instead of drive traffic
  (excerpt too long/satisfying); weak attribution; legal can't get comfortable; an unmanaged,
  brand-unsafe feed; no analytics to prove ROI.

### Publisher accounts — strategic note

Converting Annotated's central *risk* (excerpting publishers' IP — the reason the claim
button exists) into its central *moat* (being the engagement + referral channel publishers
opt into). Anchor tenants also seed credible content and solve cold-start, and a B2B layer
(verified accounts, analytics, publisher programs) opens monetization under a consumer
social product. **Cost:** it's a two-sided market — verification, dashboards, analytics,
possibly contracts — and the partner model needs a coherent excerpt/attribution policy
(always links back, excerpt-limited, so it's traffic-positive). **Scope:** post-bounty
roadmap and pitch narrative, not the bounty build.

## Cross-cutting product signals

- **The audio-clip moat is the wedge for 3 of 4** (Maya, Daniel, Priya rely on it; Marcus
  benefits). Protect podcast-source coverage aggressively — the Spotify-exclusive gap is
  the most-cited churn trigger across personas.
- **Attribution strength is existential**, not cosmetic — for Daniel and Priya it's the
  difference between "tool I can stand behind" and "legal/credibility risk."
- **A clip library / folders** is the most-requested missing capability (Priya, and Maya
  for show-prep). Currently a v1 non-goal — worth revisiting post-bounty.
- **Cold-start / audience** is the shared adoption risk (Marcus most acute). The feed only
  has value once a few sharp voices are on it.
- **Publishers are the anchor-tenant play** (Renée). Bringing newspapers/magazines on as
  verified accounts seeds credible content, solves cold-start, and flips the IP/legal risk
  into a partnership moat — the single highest-leverage post-bounty bet.
